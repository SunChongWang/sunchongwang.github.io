Chapter 2 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Epigenetic Alterations

Genes are dynamically read and utilized by cells throughout the cell cycle and in response to external stimuli. However, the accessibility and transcription of genes in the nucleus can be hindered by various factors, such as nutrient imbalances (e.g., deficiencies in folate and B vitamins), lifestyle factors (e.g., smoking, alcohol consumption, and physical inactivity), psychological stress, exposure to environmental chemicals (e.g., bisphenol A and heavy metals), and atmospheric pollutants (e.g., particulate matter). These effects are mediated by epigenetic mechanisms, including DNA methylation, histone modifications, and overproduction of miRNAs, without altering the underlying DNA sequences. Epigenetic research over the past decades has confirmed that accumulated epigenetic changes are closely associated with aging. Consequently, pronounced epigenetic alterations serve as early indicators of the onset of aging and age-related diseases (Dhar et al., 2022).

This chapter presents traditional herbal prescriptions from the GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) system that are used to address aging traits associated with epigenetic alterations. A key characteristic of aging individuals is the simultaneous presence of multiple medical conditions. Therefore, we present herbal prescriptions targeting both the aging traits and their related comorbidities. Commonly prescribed herbs and formulas for these conditions include: Jiāwèixiāoyáosǎn, Hànliáncǎo, Nǔzhēnzǐ, Qǐjúdìhuángwán, Cháihújiālónggǔmǔlìtāng, Héhuānpí, Fúlíng, Gāncǎoxiǎomàidàzǎotāng, Dānshēn, Lǐzhōngtāng, Bǔzhōngyìqìtāng, Fángfēng, Zhǐqiào, Jiégěng, Gānjiāng, Suānzǎorén, and Guìzhītāng.

2.1 Psychiatric symptom

A major psychiatric symptom in geriatrics is delirium, which is characterized by episodes of mental dysfunction including disturbances in attention, awareness, and global cognition such as memory, orientation, language, and perception. An episode of delirium occurs abruptly and can last for hours to days. The risk factors of delirium in adult patients include advanced age, surgery, and infection (Ormseth et al., 2023), all of which involve inflammation. Indeed, the DNA methylation levels at the pro-inflammatory cytokine genes in the microglia and blood samples of neurosurgical and traumatic patients were found to decrease with age; the age-related DNA methylation changes in the cytokine genes in glia and blood were thus hypothesized to contribute to the pathophysiology of delirium (Shinozaki et al., 2018), which is frequently comorbid with dementia, diabetes, and hypertension (Z. Lim et al., 2023).

2.2 Anxiety

Anxiety, a feeling of uneasiness and worry, is the emotional responses to an anticipated, uncontrollable, future threat. It is closely related to fear, which is the fight-or-flight response to a real, recognized, current threat. Examples of anxiety include the nervous emotions and behaviors of students before taking an exam, unsocial persons before meeting a stranger, and elderly patients before reading a diagnosis. If the anxiety state persists and becomes disabling even after the stressor is removed, the state turns into an anxiety disorder. It is well known that early environmental insult, such as childhood abuse, has a lingering effect on one’s entire life. Epigenetic deregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, which triggers fight-or-flight response, in anxious animals and humans has been intensely studied and summarized (Bartlett et al., 2017; Persaud & Cates, 2022). Generalized anxiety disorder is associated with depression and chronic pain (Rogers et al., 2020).

2.3 Anosmia

Anosmia is loss of the sense of all or some smells. In humans, the olfactory sensory neurons (OSNs) in the lining of the nasal cavity detect airborne and foodborne odors and then transduce electric signals to the olfactory bulb in the roof of the nasal cavity, where the olfactory signals are relayed to other parts of the forebrain, including hypothalamus and amygdala, for further processing. Although epidemiological studies consistently find increasing prevalence of anosmia and dysgeusia with age, the mechanisms remain obscure (Doty & Kamath, 2014). Nevertheless, a recent study demonstrated the downregulation of olfactory receptor (OR) genes through altered chromatin organization of the OSNs upon SARS-CoV-2 infection, and the disruptions in the OR transcription and compartmentalization remain long after viral clearance (Zazhytska et al., 2022). In addition, aging-related inflammatory changes in olfactory stem cells in nasal olfactory mucosa were shown to contribute to presbyosmia in humans (Oliva et al., 2022). Olfactory dysfunction is common in patients with Alzheimer’s disease or Parkinson’s disease (Papazian & Pinto, 2021).

2.4 Depression

Depression, a mood disorder, causes one to lose pleasure or interest in daily activities for prolonged or repeated periods of time. Childhood trauma, such as neglect and emotional abuse, was found to increase the risk of adult-onset depression (Mandelli et al., 2015). The prevalence of depression in adults was estimated at 13% (G. Y. Lim et al., 2018), while that in old people increased to 32% (Zenebe et al., 2021). Adaptive and maladaptive responses to stressful events in lifespan accumulate and can gradually disrupt the interconnectivity of the functional brain network, causing depression in later life. Epigenetics is a mechanism mediating lasting effects of the environment. DNA hypermethylation at BDNF (a nerve growth factor) and SLC6A4 (serotonin reuptake transporter) was consistently found to be associated with depression (M. Li et al., 2019). Hypertension, diabetes, and cataract were the most common physical comorbidities in old patients with depression (Grover et al., 2017).

2.5 High glucocorticoids

Glucocorticoids (cortisol in humans; corticosterone in rodents), a steroid hormone synthesized in the adrenal cortex and released into the bloodstream, regulate a range of physiological processes including blood glucose elevation and immune response suppression. The release of glucocorticoids is under the feedback control of the HPA axis in a diurnal rhythm, peaking at eight o’clock in the morning, and in response to stress. It was found that cumulative lifetime stress accelerates epigenetic aging via glucocorticoid-induced epigenetic changes in humans (Zannas et al., 2015) and the altered epigenetic landscape conduces the development of age-related diseases such as coronary artery disease and leukemias (Gassen et al., 2017).

2.6 High thyroid-stimulating hormone

Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), secreted from the pituitary gland, stimulates the thyroid gland to secrete the thyroid hormones (THs), which increase the metabolism of cells in most tissues, especially during growth, development, and stress. An increasing concentration of THs in the blood inhibits pituitary production of TSH. Levels of TSH and THs are maintained in a negative feedback loop so that high levels of TSH signify low levels of THs. A longitudinal study found that environmental chemicals (i.e., organophosphate esters (OPEs) and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)) in the blood and urine of the elderly are associated with accelerated epigenetic aging and that high TSH and low THs aggravate the contaminant-induced epigenetic aging (W. Shi et al., 2024). OPEs were shown to migrate from plastic food packaging to food (Bi et al., 2023). PAHs, produced by incomplete combustion of biomass, are ubiquitous in engine exhaust, incinerator emissions, and cigarette smoke. The common comorbidities of thyroid dysfunction include diabetes and hypertension (Rashad & Samir, 2019).

2.7 Low progesterone

Progesterone, a sexual steroid hormone produced by the ovaries/testes and adrenal glands, helps support menstruation and pregnancy in women and make sperm in men. Progesterone is protective against ovarian and endometrial carcinogenesis (Beral et al., 2008). The effects of progesterone are mediated through activation of the progesterone receptors (PRs) in the nucleus and membrane of the target cell. In women of reproductive age, the serum levels of progesterone before ovulation were found to fall slowly with increasing age (MacNaughton et al., 1992). It was also found that epigenetic repression of PR expression is linked to poor prognosis in solid tumors including endometrial, pancreatic, ovarian, and non-small cell lung cancers (Y. Li et al., 2020).
