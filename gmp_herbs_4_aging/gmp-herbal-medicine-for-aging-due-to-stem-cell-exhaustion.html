Chapter 7 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Stem Cell Exhaustion

Many tissues in the human body, such as bone marrow, the brain, skin, the intestine, cornea, muscle, testes/ovaries, and connective tissues (e.g., adipose tissue and synovium), harbor adult stem cells. These multipotent cells possess the capacity to self-renew and differentiate into specialized cell types essential for tissue maintenance and repair. However, as organisms age, the number and functional capacity of adult stem cells decline. This decline is driven by multiple factors, including DNA damage, telomere shortening, epigenetic drift, mitochondrial dysfunction, as well as alterations within the stem cell niche. These changes collectively impair regenerative abilities and contribute to age-related degeneration.

In traditional Chinese medicine, certain components of Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP)-certified herbal prescriptions are frequently employed to address aging-related phenotypes associated with stem cell exhaustion. These include: Dānshēn, Ròucōngróng, Yínyánghuò, Bājǐtiān, Jiāwèixiāoyáosǎn, Zhìgāncǎotāng, Xiānhècǎo, Sǎnzhǒngkuìjiāntāng, Xuánshēn, Guīpítāng, Hànliáncǎo, Chìsháo, Xiàkūcǎo, Tùsīzǐ, Xuèfǔzhúyūtāng, and Fùfāngdānshēnpiàn.

7.1 Alopecia

Alopecia, or hair loss, increases with age. In men, male pattern baldness is characterized by hair loss in the frontotemporal and vertex regions of the scalp, starting with a receding hairline above both temples, while in women female pattern hair loss presents a diffuse thinning of the hair across the entire scalp, starting at the hair division line. Hair starts growing at the bottom of a hair follicle at a speed of 1-1.5 cm per month. The lifespan of hair is up to seven years and new hair grows in the follicle where the old one falls away. In aged mice, hair follicle stem cells were found to escape from their niche, contributing to hair follicle miniaturization and permanent hair loss (C. Zhang et al., 2021). The percentage of people over age 65 years with hair loss was estimated at 53% in men and 37% in women (Villani et al., 2022). Male pattern baldness was reviewed to be associated with endocrine disease such as hyperandrogenemia, cancer such as prostate cancer, metabolic disease such as hypertriglyceridemia, and cardiovascular disease such as heart disease (S. Chen et al., 2022). Female pattern hair loss was found to be associated with age, menopause, and hypertension (Özkoca et al., 2022).

7.2 Ischemic heart disease

Ischemic heart disease (IHD), also called coronary artery disease or atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, is the weakening or damage of the heart due to reduced blood supply to the heart muscle. IHD manifests chest pain or discomfort which may radiate to other parts of the upper body including the left shoulder, arm, back, neck, jaw, or stomach. Poor blood supply to the heart in IHD is due mainly to narrowed coronary arteries by accumulation of atherosclerotic plaques on the inner arterial walls. Mesenchymal stem cells were shown to attenuate atherosclerosis progression through immunosuppression in mice (J. Li et al., 2019) and represent a promising therapeutic agent for the treatment of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (Gorabi et al., 2019). The global prevalence of IHD was projected to double between 2021 and 2050, reaching 5% by 2050 (H. Shi et al., 2025). IHD patients with respiratory and musculoskeletal comorbidities were found to have the highest risk of mortality (Crowe et al., 2020).

7.3 Myocardial infarction

Myocardial infarction (MI), also known as heart attack, refers to tissue death of part of the heart muscle due to lack of oxygen supply to the affected heart muscle. The cutting off of blood supply in acute MI is mostly caused by a ruptured atherosclerotic plague that forms a clot to clog the affected coronary artery. Treatment of a heart attack by pharmaceutically or surgically restoring blood supply to the affected heart muscle is crucial before more heart muscle cells die. Because heart muscle has limited regenerative capability, transplantation of autologous bone marrow stem cells after MI has attracted attention to restore heart function (W. Yan et al., 2024). The global prevalence of MI in adults aged 60 years and older was estimated at 9.5%, compared with 3.8% in adults aged 59 years and younger (Salari et al., 2023). The common non-cardiovascular comorbidities of MI were found to be chronic renal disease and anemia (Teng et al., 2020).

7.4 Low platelet count

Platelets, or thrombocytes, produced in the bone marrow, are the smallest component of the circulating blood in mammals. They function to stop bleeding by clumping to the site of vessel injury, forming a blood clot. Platelet count in old age was found to decrease by 35% in men and by 25% in women compared with early infancy (Biino et al., 2013). Not only the count but also the responsivity of platelet changes in aging (Poscablo & Forsberg, 2021). It is believed that age-related changes in hematopoietic stem cells, blood components, and vascular health altogether drive age-related changes in platelet count and function (Jones, 2016), that contribute to the thrombotic and hemorrhagic disorders in the elderly.

7.5 Urinary incontinence

Urinary incontinence is involuntary leakage of urine due to impaired bladder control. The prevalence of urinary incontinence ranged from 25% to 45%, increasing with age (Milsom & Gyhagen, 2019). An aging bladder is characterized by one that is overactive but empties ineffectively. The underlying pathology was reviewed and included deterioration of detrusor muscle function, bladder wall fibrosis, and increased sensitivity to neurotransmitters (Siroky, 2004). 7.6 Bone fracture

7.7 High parathyroid hormone

Parathyroid hormone (PTH), produced by the parathyroid glands behind the thyroid glands in the neck, regulates calcium levels in the blood. In response to low levels of serum calcium, PTH is secreted to stimulate breakdown of bones which act as a bank of calcium in the body. Serum PTH was found to increase with age regardless of vitamin D levels in normocalcemic individuals without renal dysfunction (Cavalcante et al., 2023). Note that although continual exposure to high levels of PTH causes bone resorption, intermittent exposure to controlled dose of PTH stimulates bone forming cells more than bone resorbing cells (Ponnapakkam et al., 2013). Artificially made PTH is therefore used for the treatment of osteoporosis. It was found that aging causes a decrease in the expression of PTH and PTH receptors in the dental pulp stem cells from human premolars, impairing the osteogenic differentiation ability of the dental pulp stem cells (Bhandi et al., 2021). Primary hyperparathyroidism was found to be associated with diabetes, renal stones, and osteoporosis, independent of serum vitamin D concentration (Soto-Pedre et al., 2023).
