Chapter 4 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Mitochondrial Dysfunction

Mitochondria are cellular organelles responsible for converting nutrients from food into ATP, the energy currency that powers essential cellular processes such as intra- and extracellular signaling, RNA/DNA/protein synthesis, substrate transport, neurotransmission, and muscle contraction. When mitochondria malfunction, they not only impair ATP production but also elevate the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which can compromise organ function (e.g., brain, heart, and muscle) and damage critical cellular components (e.g., DNA, lipids, and proteins). Numerous environmental agents—including heavy metals like cadmium, lead, mercury, and arsenic; chemical pollutants such as pesticides, herbicides, bisphenols, and phthalates; and substances like alcohol, antibiotics, and statins—exhibit mitochondrial toxicity (Pizzorno, 2022).

Many of the GMP-prescribed herbal formulations for addressing aging-related signs and symptoms discussed in this chapter include the following components: Dānshēn, Jìshēngshènqìwán, Zhìgāncǎotāng, Shēngmàiyǐn, Bǔyánghuánwǔtāng, Yuǎnzhì, Sìnìtāng, Niúxī, Shūjīnghuóxuètāng, Shíchāngpú, Chēqiánzǐ, Jiāwèixiāoyáosǎn, Bǔzhōngyìqìtāng, and Zhūlíngtāng. In addition to these herbal regimens, acupuncture is frequently prescribed as a complementary therapy for the health conditions described in this chapter. Research has demonstrated acupuncture’s potential to improve mitochondrial function in various contexts: for instance, it has been shown to reverse hippocampal mitochondrial dysfunction in vascular dementia rats (H. Li et al., 2016), promote cerebral mitochondrial biogenesis and neural recovery in ischemic stroke rats (K. Guo & Lu, 2024), and support muscular mitochondrial function in rats following acute swimming exercise (M. Gao et al., 2008). These findings highlight the growing recognition of acupuncture as a valuable adjunct to GMP herbal therapies in addressing age-related health challenges.

4.1 Cerebral atrophy

The volume of brain tissue in healthy individuals decreases at a rate of 0.5% per year after age 40. In the normal aging brain, the loss of gray matter tissue is region specific while the loss of white matter is widespread (Resnick et al., 2003). After 70 years of age when 14% of the brain volume has lost, the rate of brain atrophy accelerates. This increasing rate may parallel an underlying neurodegenerative disease such as Alzheimer’s disease. A common CNS manifestation of mitochondrial diseases is atrophy (Finsterer & De Carvalho, 2017). For example, a study found that mitochondrial genome variations predict whole brain and regional brain atrophy in patients with Alzheimer’s disease (Ridge et al., 2013). Besides advanced age, transient ischemic attacks, hypertension, smoking, hyperlipidemia, and excessive alcohol consumption were found to accelerate cerebral atrophy in normal aging adults (Akiyama et al., 1997).

4.2 Low cerebral blood flow

The brain consumes 20% of oxygen for normal function; adequate cerebral blood flow, which is subject to local and systemic regulation, is critical for survival. Nevertheless, cerebral blood flow was found to decrease at a rate of about 0.5% per year with increasing age; the decrease was mainly attributed to decreasing gray matter perfusion in the prefrontal cortex (Parkes et al., 2004). The decreasing cerebral blood flow is consistent with the finding of lower neuronal mitochondrial metabolism and glutamate–glutamine cycle flux in the brain of elderly adults compared with young adults (Boumezbeur et al., 2009). Furthermore, the age-related hypoperfused regions differ from the age-related atrophic regions (J. J. Chen et al., 2011), indicating that the effect of age on the brain is manifold. For example, insulin resistance and elevated BMI were found to be associated with lower regional cerebral flood flow in cognitively asymptomatic and healthy adults, respectively (Hoscheidt et al., 2017; Willeumier et al., 2011). Individuals with low cerebral blood flow are likely to have hypertension, heart disease, diabetes mellitus, and/or hyperlipidemia (Meyer et al., 1984).

4.3 Hypoxia

Hypoxia refers to low levels of oxygen in the body tissues and cells. The concentration of oxygen in the surrounding environment is sensed by the chemoreceptors in the neck and brain. The sympathetic nervous system mediates body’s response to acute hypoxia by increasing breathing and heart rates. At the cellular level, hypoxia is sensed by the hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) which activate genes to increase oxygen transport and anaerobic respiration. As mitochondria are the main consumers of oxygen in the cell, HIF pathways and mitochondria cross talk during hypoxic conditions (X. Huang et al., 2023). Although hypoxia is associated with age-related diseases such as cancer and obstructive sleep apnea (E. Yeo, 2019), moderate and controlled hypoxia has been reported to have beneficial impact on the aging neurological and cardiovascular systems (Burtscher et al., 2021; Raberin et al., 2023).

4.4 Fatigue

Fatigue is a state of low energy that is not caused by the physical and mental activities in normal life. It is described by patients as diminished endurance during physical exertion and prolonged recovery after illness. Patients with fatigue feel unrelieved after rest. Fatigue is one of the most common symptoms experienced by the elderly, with or without chronic disease. As fatigue is a hallmark symptom of mitochondrial diseases, the association between fatigue and mitochondria dysfunction was investigated and reviewed (Filler et al., 2014). Furthermore, a recent study revealed mitochondria dysfunction in the skeletal muscle of patients with long COVID presenting with post-exertional malaise (Appelman et al., 2024). The five common comorbidities of chronic fatigue syndrome are depression, anxiety disorder, insomnia, Crohn’s disease, and ulcerative colitis (Leong et al., 2022).

4.5 High resting heart rate

Low resting heart rate is known to be associated with health and longevity, while increasing resting heart rate over time is associated with increasing risk of adverse events (Jensen, 2019). Cardiomyocytes stop proliferating soon after birth and the heart grows through increase in the size of cardiomyocytes throughout adolescence. During adulthood, cardiac stress from e.g., exercise or hypertension causes loss of some cardiomyocytes, which hardly regenerate. The pathologic cardiac stress accumulates over time, leading to mitochondrial dysfunction, cardiomyocyte death, inflammation, fibrosis, and functional decline of the heart later in life (Mendoza & Karch, 2022). A study found that each 15 beats per minute increase in the resting heart rate increases the risk of cardiovascular disease mortality by a factor of 1.2 in healthy men and 1.3 in healthy women (Cooney et al., 2010). 4.6 Ventricular hypertrophy

Ventricular hypertrophy refers to thickening of the walls of one or both of the lower chambers of the heart called ventricles. The right ventricle pumps blood to the lung and the left ventricle to the rest of the body. If the blood pressure or blood volume to the ventricle increase during growth, pregnancy, or exercise, the muscles in the ventricular walls grow to overcome the increased workload from the vascular system. If the workload persists in, e.g., systemic hypertension and there is no sufficient proliferation of the vasculature in the hypertrophic ventricle, cardiomyocyte death, inflammation, and fibrosis can occur, leading to malignant arrhythmia and heart failure. Pathological left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), more common than right ventricular hypertrophy, results from maladaptive responses of the left ventricle to blood pressure overload. Mitochondria occupy about one third of the cardiomyocyte volume to supply 90% of the cardiomyocyte ATP. Alterations in mitochondrial morphology, biogenesis, dynamics, and bioenergetics during cardiac hypertrophy in cardiac hypertrophy were extensively studied and reviewed (D. Yang et al., 2022). The prevalence of LVH was 9% and individuals with LVH were older and likely to have hypertension, diabetes, or cardiovascular disease (Taki et al., 2023).

4.7 Low epinephrine

4.10 Neuropathy

Neuropathy, or peripheral neuropathy, refers to damage or disease of the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord, causing pain or numbness in the part of the affected nerves. Damage to mitochondria via trauma, chemotherapy drugs, hyperglycemia, and viral infection was reviewed to result in cellular apoptosis and contribute to the pathogenesis of neuropathies (Khuankaew et al., 2020). 4.11 Edema

Edema is swelling due to accumulated interstitial fluid in body tissues such as the feet, ankles, legs, abdomen, hands, arms, and face. The risk of peripheral edema increases with age, with a prevalence of ~20% among adults over 50 years old (Besharat et al., 2021). Edema is a common symptom of heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, or cirrhosis, the latter of which is characterized by such mitochondria dysfunction as increased mitochondrial ROS production; increased mitochondrial calcium concentration; and reduced mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (Middleton & Vergis, 2021). Diabetes, heart failure, and reduced mobility are the common comorbidities of chronic edema in elderly patients (Moffatt et al., 2019).

4.12 Varicose veins

Varicose veins are enlarged, elongated, and twisted veins under the skin on the legs, ankles, and feet. The prevalence of varicose veins increases with age due to the aging venous system (Beebe-Dimmer et al., 2005). Both the total mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and proportion of no-deletion mtDNA were found to be lower in the varicose vein tissue samples than non-varicose vein tissue samples from patients with varicose veins (Smetanina et al., 2022). The presence of varicose veins is associated with an increased risk of developing deep venous thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and peripheral artery disease (S. Chang et al., 2018).

4.13 Hypothyroidism

Hypothyroidism is a condition where one’s thyroid gland does not make enough thyroid hormones (TH), which enhance body metabolism by stimulating mitochondria respiration and mitochondria biogenesis of the target cells. The prevalence of hypothyroidism increases with age due to increasing serum antithyroid autoantibodies with age (Hollowell et al., 2002).
