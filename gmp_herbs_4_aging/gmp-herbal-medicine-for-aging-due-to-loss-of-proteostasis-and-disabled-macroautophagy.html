Chapter 3 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Loss of Proteostasis and Disabled Macroautophagy

The life cycle of proteins begins with the folding of amino acid sequences into functional three-dimensional structures. These proteins are then transported to specific intracellular or extracellular compartments where they perform their biological roles. When misfolded, aggregated, or no longer required, they are degraded by cellular quality control systems. As the workhorses of the cell, maintaining protein homeostasis (proteostasis) relies on a complex network of molecular machineries, including molecular chaperones, the proteasome, and (macro)autophagy pathways. However, proteostasis can be overwhelmed by factors such as spontaneous genetic mutations, oxidative stress, and persistent environmental insults—including exposure to extreme temperatures, cold stress, dehydration, ultraviolet radiation, heavy metals, alcohol, pH fluctuations, and infections. Over time, the accumulation of misfolded and damaged proteins leads to cellular dysfunction and age-related pathologies, such as neurodegenerative diseases (Labbadia & Morimoto, 2015).

In traditional Chinese medicine, GMP-certified herbs and formulas are frequently employed to address medical conditions linked to impaired protein quality control systems. These include: Yuǎnzhì, Dānshēn, Zīshènmíngmùtāng, Qǐjúdìhuángwán, Shíchāngpú, Juémíngzǐ, Jiāwèixiāoyáosǎn, Bǔyánghuánwǔtāng, Tùsīzǐ, Ròucōngróng, Sháoyàogāncǎotāng, Yìqìcōngmíngtāng, Chēqiánzǐ, Nǔzhēnzǐ, Gǔjīngcǎo, and Zhìgāncǎotāng.

3.1 Cataract

A cataract is an opaque region in the eye lens. The eye lens is composed predominantly of crystallin proteins, which help maintain proper refractivity and transparency of the lens. The protein structure of alpha-crystallin, one of the three crystallin subtypes in vertebrates, is similar to that of small heat-shock proteins; alpha-crystallin has thus chaperone-like properties, preventing precipitation of misfolded proteins and formation of large light-scattering aggregates in cataractous lens. Lens proteins including alpha-crystallin, albeit long-lived, denature and degrade over time. Defective alpha-crystallin was shown to alter lens morphology, autophagy, and stress responses, leading to lens opacification in aged mice with alpha-crystallin mutant knock-in (Andley & Goldman, 2016). The common systemic comorbidities in patients undergoing cataract surgery were identified to be hypertension and diabetes (Mishrikotkar et al., 2021).

3.2 Visual acuity decrease

Presbyopia is one of the common age-related visual acuity impairments besides cataracts. Presbyopia, also known as age-related farsightedness, is the inability to focus clearly on close objects because of increased stiffness of the eye lens. It was shown that presbyopia results from a loss of alpha-crystallin in the center of the lens together with progressive heat-induced denaturation of structural proteins in the lens (Heys et al., 2007). Another common age-related visual acuity impairment is myodesopsias or vitreous floaters. The vitreous is the transparent gel-like fluid that fills the space between the lens and the retina of the eyeball. Molecular alterations in the vitreous such as aggregation of collagen fibrils can form fibers that scatter light and cast tiny shadows called floaters on the retina. Musculoskeletal conditions or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease exacerbate the decline in health-related quality-of-life in visually impaired old patients (Van Nispen et al., 2009).

3.3 Hearing loss

Presbycusis is progressive, irreversible decline in hearing sensitivity due to age-related degeneration of the auditory system, especially the cochlea in the inner ear. Sound vibrations cause bending or deflection of the cilia of the hair cells in the cochlea. The function of the hair cells is to amplify and convert the acoustic stimuli into electrochemical signals, which are relayed via the auditory nerve to the auditory cortex in the temporal lobe of the brain for further analysis. Damages, e.g., noise and ototoxicity, to the auditory cells, tissues, and their microenvironments accumulate over time and contribute to presbycusis. It was shown in old rats that upregulation of autophagy inhibits apoptosis to promote survival of inner ear hair cells (Z. He et al., 2021) and to slow degeneration of auditory cortex neurons (J. Yuan et al., 2018). Presbycusis is linked to depression and dementia (Bowl & Dawson, 2019).

3.4 Alzheimer’s disease

Alzheimer’s disease (AD), accounting for about 60% of dementia cases worldwide, is the leading type of dementia. The most common symptoms of AD are loss of short-term memory and difficulty in finding words, first appearing around 65 years of age. AD is believed to be caused by aggregates of intracellular hyperphosphorylated tau proteins (called tau tangles) and extracellular misfolded beta-amyloid (called amyloid plaques) in the hippocampus and cerebral cortex responsible for memory, language, reasoning, and social behaviors. The global prevalence of the AD continuum, including prodromal AD, preclinical AD, and AD, was estimated at 22% of all persons aged 50 and above (Gustavsson et al., 2022). Depression, cerebrovascular disease, osteoporosis, and diabetes were found to be common comorbidities in AD (J. Wang, Wu, et al., 2018).

3.5 Parkinsonism

Parkinsonism is a set of motor symptoms, including slowed movements, tremors, rigidity, and unstable posture, that occurs in a range of conditions including Parkinson’s disease (PD). PD is caused by aggregates of misfolded alpha-synuclein proteins in the cytoplasm of the dopamine-releasing neurons in the region of the midbrain involved in reward and movement. Aberrant alpha-synuclein can transmit to damage neighboring cells, contributing to disease propagation. Advanced stages of PD manifest nonmotor symptoms such as depression, constipation, and sleep disorders. PD affects 1% of the population above 60 years old (Tysnes & Storstein, 2017).

3.6 Seizures

Seizures are abnormal, excessive, and synchronized electric activities of the neurons in the brain, resulting in changes in movements and/or consciousness. An episode of seizure can last for seconds to minutes. A seizure can be provoked by high fever, central nervous system infection, head trauma, electrocyte imbalance, medication use/withdrawal, and so on. Repeated unprovoked seizures are called epilepsy. The age distribution of epilepsy prevalence shows bimodal peaks at the two ends: pediatric patients and geriatric patients (Beghi & Giussani, 2018). Among the elders, most seizures are secondary to epileptogenic conditions such as stroke and dementia, while new-onset seizures are due to alcohol withdrawal, strokes, and electrolyte disturbances (Zagaria, 2008).
