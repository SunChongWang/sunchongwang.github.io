Real-World Evidence-Based Herbal Medicine for Aging

Chapter 1 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Genomic Instability and Telomere Attrition

A genome encompasses all DNA sequences—both nuclear and mitochondrial—that encode the instructions necessary for an organism’s development, function, and survival. The integrity of the genome can be compromised by endogenous factors such as DNA replication/repair errors, telomere attrition, spontaneous mutations, and reactive oxygen species (ROS), as well as exogenous agents like ionizing radiation, ultraviolet (UV) light, and environmental mutagens (e.g., acetaldehyde, aflatoxins, arsenic, asbestos, benzene, formaldehyde, oncoviruses, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, and tobacco-specific nitrosamines). These insults can lead to genomic instability. While changes in DNA sequences introduce genetic diversity, which enhances the adaptive capacity of microorganisms to environmental challenges, genome conservation is critical for maintaining the structural and functional integrity of multicellular organisms. Throughout evolution, a delicate balance between genomic diversity and conservation has been fine-tuned to ensure survival across the tree of life. However, evidence suggests that this equilibrium is disrupted in aging cells. For instance, DNA base excision repair efficiency declines in human cells with age (Atamna et al., 2000), and telomeres in adult leukocytes shorten by approximately 25 base pairs annually (Müezzinler et al., 2013).

The concept of “aging hallmarks”—a set of biological processes central to human aging—was first proposed by López-Otín et al. (2013) and later expanded upon in subsequent studies (López-Otín, Blasco, et al., 2023). These hallmarks include critical processes such as genome instability and telomere attrition. Building on this framework, Andreassen et al. (2019) identified signs and symptoms associated with these aging hallmarks. Concurrently, S.-C. Wang (2023) systematically elucidated the clinical application of Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP)-certified herbal prescriptions for addressing signs, symptoms, and diseases.

Based on these findings, it is now possible to develop targeted GMP herbal prescriptions tailored to specific aging phenotypes associated with individual hallmarks. The GMP herbs and formulations included in the prescriptions discussed in this chapter are as follows: Guīpítāng, Fúshén, Yèjiāoténg, Suānzǎoréntāng, Dānshēn, Tiānwángbǔxīndān, Xuèfǔzhúyūtāng, Bǔzhōngyìqìtāng, Tǔfúlíng, Héshǒuwū, Hànliáncǎo, Nǔzhēnzǐ, Yuǎnzhì, Qībǎoměirándān, Rénshēnyǎngróngtāng, Xiāngshāliùjūnzǐtāng, and Sǎnzhǒngkuìjiāntāng.

1.1 Graying of hair

Gray hair is one of the most visible and also earliest signs of aging in humans. Hair color is determined by melanin pigments produced by the melanocytes in hair follicles. Defects in the synthesis of melanin, due, e.g., to oxidative stress-induced melanocyte apoptosis or senescence, drive graying of hair. Cumulative intrafollicular free radicals also disrupt maintenance of melanocyte stem cells residing in the bulge of hair follicles and depletion of the melanocyte stem cells perpetuates hair graying (O’Sullivan et al., 2020). Although the onset age of hair graying varies from person to person, non-genetic risk factors such as hormone imbalance, psychoemotional stress, nutrient deficiencies, UV radiation, tobacco smoking, and medication have been implicated to play a role in graying initiation. A systematic review and meta-analysis found that people with premature graying of hair are likely to be obese or hypertensive (Mahendiratta et al., 2020).

1.2 Facial winkles

Skin aging is characterized by slower turnover of the keratinocytes in the epidermis that results in slow wound healing and less desquamation of the epidermis; depletion and disorganization of the extracellular matrix proteins, including collagen and elastin, in the dermis which give rise wrinkles; and site-specific loss of fat and laxity of facial muscles in the hypodermis which contributes to facial sagging. Both collagen and elastin are synthesized and secreted by the fibroblasts in the dermis. Accumulated DNA damage to the fibroblasts due to endogenous and UV-induced ROS can cause fibroblast senescence, exacerbating dermal aging. In vivo quantification of oxidative stress in human facial skin was developed and oxidative stress was shown to correlate with age and wrinkle score in areas surrounding the eye (Tsuchida & Kobayashi, 2020). Looking younger was found to be associated with less chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and osteoporosis (Mekić et al., 2023).

1.3 Telangiectasia

Telangiectasias, also known as spider veins, refer to prominent, dilated small blood vessels (arterioles, venules, or capillaries) near the surface of the skin, often on the face or legs. Arteriolar (venular) telangiectasias are red (blue) in color. The dilated vessels are small in diameter (< 1mm) and thus do not protrude above the skin surface. Capillary telangiectasias are initially small and red but can become wide and blue with time. Telangiectasias are generally asymptomatic and represent primarily as a cosmetic concern. The etiology of telangiectasias is not clear. However, as the risk factors of telangiectasias include sunlight exposure, age, temperature extremes, and hormonal fluctuations, weakness of the blood vessel walls due to age-related DNA damage may play a role in chronic venous insufficiency such as telangiectasis (Ortega et al., 2021). The comorbidities of chronic venous disease include emphysema and diabetes (Matic et al., 2015).

1.4 Anemia

Anemia is a lower-than-normal number of red blood cells or hemoglobin, which carry oxygen around the body. About a quarter of the global population was anemic; and children under the age of 5 years and women of reproductive age were most affected due to dietary iron deficiency and gynecological disorders, respectively (GBD 2021 Anaemia Collaborators, 2023). In healthy middle-aged adults, the telomere length of peripheral blood leucocytes was found to be positively correlated with red blood cell counts and this correlation was more significant in adults aged 45 years and older (De Meyer et al., 2008). Anemia in the elderly was associated with cardiovascular diseases and cognitive impairments (Stauder & Thein, 2014).

1.5 Low total lymphocytes

Lymphocytes, derived from the hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow, are a type of white blood cells that fight off infections and diseases. Lymphocytes consist of helper T-cells, cytotoxic T-cells, B-cells, and natural killer (NK) cells; the former three are involved in the cell-mediated, cytotoxic, and humoral adaptive immunity respectively, and the last are part of cell-mediated, cytotoxic innate immunity. The total lymphocytes were found to decline progressively from infancy, adolescence, adulthood to old age although the number of NK cells increased in the elderly (Valiathan et al., 2016). Fewer lymphocytes compromise immunity. Shorter leukocyte telomere length was indeed found to be associated with a higher risk of infection and pneumonia (Helby et al., 2017).

1.6 Cerebellar atrophy

The cerebellum, located at the hindbrain above the brain stem, is responsible for the precision, timing, and learning of muscle movements. The total cerebellar volume was found to shrink after 50 years of age (Luft et al., 1999) and the age-related cerebellar shrinking depends on cerebellar regions and sex (Y. Wang, Teng, et al., 2024). A dramatic increase in non-random DNA double-strand breaks and aneuploidy was found in the cerebellum of patients with ataxia telangiectasia, which is a rare genetic disorder featuring cerebellar atrophy. Because the cerebellum, an integral part of the brain functional network, connects to the cerebral cortex and limbic system, cerebellar dysfunction also causes a constellation of cognitive and affective deficits including executive function impairment, spatial recognition difficulties, language deficits, and personality changes (Schmahmann & Sherman, 1998). Region-specific cerebellar atrophy is seen in early stages of dementia subtypes including Alzheimer’s disease, diffuse neocortical Lewy body disease, and frontotemporal lobar degeneration (Y. 1.7 Low melatonin

1.8 Cancer

Cancerous cells not only proliferate rapidly and indefinitely but also spread through bloodstream or lymphatic system to other parts of the body such as the bones, liver, and lungs. Crushing or blocking of the invaded and metastasized cells on the nearby and distal parts of the originating site cause cancer morbidity and mortality. Cancer is the leading cause of death; it was estimated that approximately one in five people develop cancer in a lifetime, whereas around one in nine men and one in 12 women die from it (Bray et al., 2024). Damage of DNA, especially in the tumor suppressor genes, initiates carcinogenesis; therefore, genome instability is called enabling hallmark of cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg, 2000). However, telomere attrition and stem cell exhaustion suppress oncogenesis. The agonistic and antagonistic relationship between aging hallmarks and cancer hallmarks may explain the observed age-related increase and oldest age-related decrease in cancer morbidity and mortality (López-Otín, Pietrocola, et al., 2023).
