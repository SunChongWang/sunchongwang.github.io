Real-World Evidence-Based Herbal Medicine for Aging

Preface

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), the global population aged 60 years and older is projected to double from 2020 to 2050, reaching 2.1 billion, while those aged 80 and above will triple to 0.4 billion.1 By 2050, these age groups will account for 22% and 4% of the world population, respectively. This demographic shift is driven by increased life expectancy and declining birth rates, leading to an overall rise in median age and accelerated population aging. While people are living longer, their healthspan—the period during which they remain healthy—has not significantly increased, resulting in older individuals spending their additional years in poor health. This aging population presents a particularly challenging issue for developing countries in Asia and Africa, which often lack the resources to effectively address these demographic changes.

A key epidemiological characteristic of geriatrics is multimorbidity,2 where elderly patients typically suffer from multiple concurrent diseases. The traditional paradigm of drug development in modern Western medicine (MWM) focuses on a one drug-one target/disease approach, which may not be well-suited for older patients with complex health needs. As a result, reviewing polypharmacy-related adverse effects has become a routine part of geriatric assessments within MWM. Since aging is influenced by a combination of genetics, behavior, and environmental factors, the varied aging trajectories highlight the need for patient-tailored medicine for the elderly.

Historically, ancient healers relied on natural products, particularly plant roots and rhizomes, to alleviate various conditions such as pain, fatigue, and insomnia, despite lacking an understanding of genetics and cellular biology. Over time, these natural remedies were combined into decoctions designed to address multiple conditions across different organ systems, such as the lungs and skin. Today, many traditional decoctions have been refined into concentrated granules by Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP)-certified manufacturers,3 akin to the processing of fresh milk into instant milk powder. This book presents prescriptions utilizing these granulated herbal products, available as dietary supplements in the U.S., aimed at relieving the health conditions of the elderly and extending their healthspan.

Approach and Features

Aging is a complex process, but since 2013 researchers have illuminated various hallmarks of aging—aging mechanisms that operate at the molecular, cellular, and systemic levels.4 Aging phenotypes associated with each hallmark have been identified through the co-occurrence of these phenotypes and hallmarks in research articles indexed in the PubMed database of the National Library of Medicine, National Institutes of Health, U.S.5

We then associate these aging phenotypes with herbal prescriptions using a state-of-the-art AI model trained on a decade’s worth of medical claims data from Taiwan,6 whose medical insurance program has reimbursed GMP-certified granular herbal prescriptions since its establishment in 1995. Importantly, the herbal prescriptions featured in this book are derived from real-world usage data provided by licensed traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) physicians, rather than from texts written by ancient healers.

Our Transformer-based AI model shares similarities with ChatGPT but differs in that its data inputs and expected outputs are clearly defined. This structured approach prevents the AI model from generating inaccuracies or “hallucinations.” The model’s generalization capability allows it to recommend treatments for a wide range of disease combinations. Because it is built on the collective insights of approximately 5,000 TCM physicians who contributed to the medical claims data, the prescriptions in this book represent a broad spectrum of expertise, rather than specialized focus of individual practitioners as seen in most other sources.

Organization of the book

The book explores 86 common traits associated with human aging, organized into distinct sections. Each section begins with an introduction to the trait, followed by a table that lists comorbid signs or diseases alongside their corresponding GMP herbal prescriptions. At the end of each section, the author provides annotations for the herbs mentioned in the table, citing the latest peer-reviewed pharmacologic studies—both clinical and preclinical—related to the constituent herbs and formulas.

aging phenotype (ICD9 / ICD10) + comorbid condition (ICD9 / ICD10)

Herb1’ 5g + herb2’ 5g + herb3’ 5-0g + …

where

  • the aging condition is followed by the disease codes7 in parentheses

  • a GMP herbal prescription consists typically of a mixture of multiple GMP granulated herbal formulas and multiple GMP granulated herbs, or of multiple GMP granulated herbs. The latter are called single-herb prescriptions and are in grey colored background.

  • Formula1’ 5g + formula2’ 4g + … + herb1’ 2-1g + herb2’ 0g + … indicates an alternative prescription

  • Formula and herb names are in pinyin8

  • the dose (in gram) of the constituent GMP formula or herb follows the name of the formula and herb

  • 2-1g indicates that the dose is between 2 and 1 gram; 0g indicates that the dose is less than 1 gram
