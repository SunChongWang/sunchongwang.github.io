Chapter 5 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Cellular Senescence

Cellular senescence is a state of irreversible cell cycle arrest. Although senescent cells are no longer capable of division, they remain metabolically active and exert paracrine effects on their microenvironment through the release of senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) factors. These factors include inflammatory cytokines, immune modulators, growth factors, and proteases, which can influence neighboring cells. Transient SASP has been implicated in processes such as embryonic development, tissue repair, and wound healing (W. Huang et al., 2022). However, prolonged senescence—induced by telomere shortening, oxidative stress, and DNA damage caused by radiation or genotoxic agents—ultimately leads to chronic inflammation and tissue dysfunction.

Traditional Chinese medicine treatments such as Xuèfǔzhúyūtāng, Dānshēn, Dúhuójìshēngtāng, Shūjīnghuóxuètāng, Chēqiánzǐ, Yùjīn, Tiānmágōuténgyǐn, Zhūlíngtāng, acupuncture, Zhìgāncǎotāng, Yìqìcōngmíngtāng, Jìshēngshènqìwán, and Shíhú are frequently used to address aging phenotypes linked to cellular senescence. Notably, acupuncture has demonstrated efficacy in this context, as evidenced by a study showing that electroacupuncture prevents stress-induced premature senescence in nucleus pulposus cells of rabbits with degenerative intervertebral discs (M. Wang et al., 2025).

5.1 Kyphosis

Kyphosis is an excessive outward curving of the thoracic spine resulting in a bowing or rounding of the upper back. After the age of 40 years, the degree of kyphosis in ~30% of adults increases with age at a rate of about 0.5 degree per year and the rate of increase is higher in females than in males (Katzman et al., 2010). In aging, some of the cell types in the bone and intervertebral disc microenvironment become senescent, causing bone loss and disc degeneration (X. He et al., 2023; Silwal et al., 2023). Kyphosis would result if aggregated senescent cells take a toll on the thoracic spine. Kyphosis is associated with osteoporotic vertebral fracture and decline in lung function (Lorbergs et al., 2017; Van Der Jagt-Willems et al., 2015).

5.2 Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis is characterized by a decreased bone density, rendering the bone weak and fragile. The bone is a living tissue; old and micro-damaged bones are dissolved and new bones are created in the place of the old ones in a process called bone remodeling. In aging, the number and activity of the bone formation cells decline while the activity of the bone resorption cells remain, leading to osteoporosis. In the bone microenvironment of aging mice, the senescent cells and their SASP that contribute to the imbalanced bone homeostasis were identified (Farr et al., 2016). The prevalence of osteoporosis in adults aged 50 years and older was found to be 9%; females were more likely to have osteoporosis; and arthrosis and arthritis were the top two comorbidities of age-related osteoporosis (Puth et al., 2018).

5.3 Arteriosclerosis

Arteriosclerosis is hardening and stiffening of the walls of the arteries that carry oxygen-rich blood from the heart to the rest of the body. The middle (i.e. muscular) layer of the arterial walls that determines the biomechanical property of the arteries comprises collagen, elastin, and vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs). In aging, the elastic fibers can fracture due to material fatigue, reducing vascular elasticity. Furthermore, senescent VSMCs found in aged vessels are replaced by fibrotic scars, augmenting age-related arterial stiffening (Schellinger et al., 2019). Arterial stiffening was found to be more severe in men than in premenopausal women; more severe in postmenopausal women than in men; and associated with chronic kidney disease and diabetes (Y. Lu et al., 2020). Atherosclerosis, a type of arteriosclerosis, is caused by a buildup of plaque made of fatty substances on the inner arterial walls, hardening and narrowing the arteries. Senescent vascular endothelial cells, senescent VSMCs, and senescent macrophages are involved in the formation and development of atherosclerosis (C. Wu et al., 2020).

5.4 Calcification

Calcification is the deposition of calcium salts in a body tissue such as bone in normal growth or vascular walls in pathological aging. Calcium ions (Ca2+), the most versatile cellular messenger in signal transduction, regulate cellular activities through organized cytosolic Ca2+ concentration, kinetics, and spatial localization in response to extrinsic and intrinsic stimuli in every cell of the body. Disrupted cellular Ca2+ signaling and/or Ca2+ homeostasis can have pathological outcomes. A rise in intracellular Ca2+ accompanies cellular senescence (Martin & Bernard, 2018). Studies have elucidated the role of cellular senescent in vascular calcification (Mackenzie & MacRae, 2011; Mas-Bargues et al., 2022) and valvular calcification (Molnár et al., 2022). The abdominal aorta starts to calcify at age of 40 years and then the ascending thoracic aorta calcifies later in life; BMI and hypertension are among the risk factors of vascular calcification (Jadidi et al., 2021).

5.5 Declining renal function

The kidneys filter about 200 liters of blood every day, from which about 2 liters of urine containing toxins, wastes, ions, and water are formed and removed. The macroscopic changes of the kidney with age are characterized by a decrease in the volume of renal parenchyma and scarring of the renal surface, while the age-related microscopic kidney changes include global glomerulosclerosis, focal tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis. The structural changes result in functional decline of the kidney. For example, 5-10% decrease in the glomerular filtration rate (GFR) per decade after age 35 years was reported (Glassock & Rule, 2012). The declining renal function also correlates with increase in the senescent load of the renal tubular endothelial cells and glomerular podocytes (Rex et al., 2023). Hypertension, obesity, ischemic heart disease, and diabetes were found to be commonly comorbid with decreased renal function (Hirst et al., 2021).

5.6 High angiotensin

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) involves multiple organs of the body, including the kidney, liver, lung, arterioles, and pituitary gland, to regulate blood pressure and blood volume, through constriction of arteriolar walls and retention of water and salt in the body, on a long-term basis. Overactivation of the RAAS can lead to hypertension, cardiovascular events, and chronic kidney disease. Angiotensin II, produced in vascular tissues, is the major bioactive product of the RAAS, and was demonstrated to promote vascular inflammation by inducing premature senescence of vascular smooth muscle cells in vascular aging (Kunieda et al., 2006). Angiotensin II levels were found to correlate with degree of depression in patients with coronary artery disease (W. Han et al., 2020).

5.7 Glaucoma

Glaucoma is an eye condition due to damage to the optic nerve in the back of the eye that transmit visual information to the brain. Old eyes were shown to lose more retinal ganglion cells that make up the optic nerve than young eyes for the same level of intraocular pressure (Jammal et al., 2020). After adjusting for the age dependence, glaucoma was found to be the second leading cause of blindness worldwide (Burton et al., 2021). The roles of accumulated senescent retinal ganglion cells in the development of glaucoma were reviewed (Y. Zhang, Huang, et al., 2024). Overweight/obesity, migraine, asthma, and smoking increase the risk of conversion from ocular hypertension to open-angle glaucoma, the most common clinical subtype of glaucoma (Garcia-Villanueva et al., 2022).
