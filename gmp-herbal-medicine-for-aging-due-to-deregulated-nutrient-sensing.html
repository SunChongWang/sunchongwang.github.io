Chapter 6 GMP Herbal Medicine for Aging due to Deregulated Nutrient-sensing

The body relies on energy and essential materials from food to sustain vital physiological processes, including respiration, cognition, waste elimination, tissue growth, cellular repair, and energy storage. A sophisticated network of biochemical pathways has evolved to monitor and respond to fluctuations in nutrient levels—such as glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids—orchestrating shifts between cellular recycling/repair during nutrient scarcity and synthesis/growth during periods of abundance. However, prolonged nutrient overload, driven by factors like excessive sugar consumption, high-fat diets, sedentary behavior, or circadian rhythm disruption, suppresses cellular repair mechanisms and heightens oxidative stress (a consequence of metabolic byproducts). This imbalance accelerates cellular damage and contributes to organismal aging.

Frequently prescribed constituents in GMP herbal prescriptions for aging symptoms associated with deregulated nutrient-sensing pathways include: Dānshēn, Shānzhā, Yuǎnzhì, Bǔyánghuánwǔtāng, Shíchāngpú, Jiāwèixiāoyáosǎn, Fángfēngtōngshèngsǎn, Héshǒuwū, Liùwèidìhuángwán, Shūjīnghuóxuètāng, Juémíngzǐ, and Suānzǎoréntāng.

Single-herb GMP prescriptions targeting deregulated nutrient-sensing aging traits commonly include: Dàhuáng, Chìsháo, Gāncǎo, Dìhuáng, Huángqí, Huánglián, Báizhú, Fúlíng, Huángqín, Zhǐshí, Xuánshēn, Héshǒuwū, Fùzǐ, Dāngguī, Dānshēn, Dǎngshēn, and Báisháo.

6.1 Obesity

Obesity is a condition where fats in the body accumulate to the extent that one’s health is compromised. A measure to quantify obesity is when one’s body mass index (BMI) exceeds 30 kg/m2. A multi-cohort epidemiological study found that obesity better predicts aging hallmark related diseases than other risk factors including education, dietary factors, smoking, physical activity, alcohol consumption, and depression, and that obesity most likely cooccurs with diseases related to deregulated nutrient sensing (Kivimäki et al., 2024). It was found that three out of every four patients in the United States were overweight with a BMI greater than 25 kg/m2 and that increasing BMI was associated with diabetes, prediabetes, and hypertension (Pantalone et al., 2017).

6.2 Unintentional weight loss

Unintentional weight loss is involuntary loss of 10% or more of the total body weight over 5-10 years or after age 50 years. Unintentional weight loss is encountered in about 8% of adult outpatients and 27% of frail people aged 65 years and over and is associated with an increased mortality (Alibhai et al., 2005). The causes of unintentional weight loss include malignancy, depression, nonmalignant gastrointestinal disease, and endocrine disorder. However, about a quarter of the elderly with unintentional weight loss have no obvious medical causes. Among the cancers associated with weight loss, the top ten cancer were found to be prostate, colorectal, lung, gastroesophageal, pancreatic, non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma, ovarian, multiple myeloma, renal tract, and biliary tree (B. D. Nicholson et al., 2018). In the elderly, nonmalignant diseases are more common than malignant diseases to cause weight loss. Infection, medication, and polypharmacy that interrupt taste or cause nausea can also result in unintentional weight loss in the elderly.

6.3 Diabetes mellitus

Diabetes mellitus is characterized by sustained high levels of glucose in the blood, which over time leads to serious health complications by damaging the cardiovascular system, eyes, kidneys, and nerves. The cause of diabetes is insufficient production of and/or defective responsiveness to insulin which is the major hormone in the body to regulate blood glucose levels. The global age-standardized prevalence of diabetes was 6% in 2021; half of the global diabetes burden was attributable to high BMI; and the global age-standardized diabetic prevalence was projected to rise to over 13% by 2050 (GBD 2021 Diabetes Collaborators, 2023). Lifestyle modifications, such as low carbohydrate diet and physical activity, were found to be more effective than pharmacological interventions to reverse prediabetes (Galaviz et al., 2022).

6.4 Hypertriglyceridemia

Hypertriglyceridemia is high levels of triglycerides in the blood. Triglycerides are the most common type of fat in the body, coming from foods, such as butter and oils, or from extra calories. Consistent high levels of triglycerides in the blood mix with other fatty substances in the blood, such as cholesterol, sticking to the walls of the arteries to cause atherosclerosis. Too much fat can also build up in the liver and pancreas, causing nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and inflammation of the pancreas (Pedersen et al., 2016; Tomizawa et al., 2014). A fifth of the global female adult population and more than a third of the global male population had hypertriglyceridemia (Grupo de Investigación del Estudio SIMETAP, 2020).

6.5 Low serum albumin

Serum albumin, produced in the liver, is the most abundant circulating protein in plasma. The major function of serum albumin is to keep a balanced distribution of fluids in the body. Low serum albumin, i.e., hypoalbuminemia, can manifest fluid-induced swelling of the legs, buildup of fluid in the abdomen, and collection of fluid in hollow organs. 6.9 Low brain glutamate

Glutamate, an amino acid, is the most abundant excitatory neurotransmitter in the central nervous system. Upon receipt of a nerve impulse, glutamate is released from the vesicles in the axon terminal into synaptic cleft for the binding by glutamate receptors in the postsynaptic neurons. Part of the glutamate in the synapse is taken up by the neighboring astrocyte where glutamate is converted into glutamine. Glutamine in the astrocyte is shuttled back to the neuron. The glutamine entering the neuron is converted to glutamate and repackaged in the presynaptic vesicles, completing the glutamate-glutamine cycle. Glutamate levels were found to decrease in normal aged adults and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) patients (D. Huang et al., 2017; Roalf et al., 2020). Lower levels of prefrontal glutamate were shown to contribute to working memory deficits and decision-making impairment in older adulthood (Rmus et al., 2023).
